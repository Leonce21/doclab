Understanding Sickle Cell Anemia: Causes, Symptoms, and Diagnosis

Sickle cell anemia is a genetic blood disorder that affects the shape and function of red blood cells. It is caused by a mutation in the HBB gene, which provides instructions for making beta-globin, a protein component of hemoglobin. Hemoglobin is responsible for carrying oxygen throughout the body, and the mutation causes the red blood cells to become rigid and sickle-shaped, which can lead to blockages in blood vessels and reduced oxygen supply to tissues and organs.

Symptoms of sickle cell anemia can vary between individuals, but some of the commonly noted symptoms include:

  1. Anemia or decreased number of red blood cells and oxygen
  2. Irritability or fussiness in babies
  3. Severe fatigue or tiredness
  4. Frequent episodes of pain, also known as crises, in different parts of the body
  5. Swelling of hands and feet, associated with pain

Sickle cell anemia is diagnosed through blood tests, which can detect the presence of sickle cell hemoglobin. Prenatal screening may be performed using amniotic fluid, and newborn screening is performed by heel prick test. The doctor may also ask about family history and perform a physical exam to check for symptoms like enlargement of the spleen and pain.

There is no cure for sickle cell anemia, but treatments such as medications and blood transfusions aim to manage the symptoms and prevent crises. Some of the commonly used medications include ibuprofen, hydroxyurea, and L-glutamine. In severe cases, bone marrow transplantation may be considered.

It is important to seek medical attention immediately if you or someone you know is exhibiting symptoms of sickle cell anemia. There is no prevention for sickle cell anemia, but people with this condition who are planning for a baby may consult a genetic counselor for preventive measures.
